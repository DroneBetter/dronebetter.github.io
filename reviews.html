Conway & Guy, The Book of Numbers (1996)

I like both of the authors but overall strongly dislike, in that it feels as though it dangles results above the reader's head without explaining the requisite tools to obtain them; it is a book for maths tourism, not a handbook.

In particular, those I find most egregious/otherwise notable

p. 258, on harmonic numbers
this is a rare moment of lucidity; they offer up a closed form for iterated partial sums of harmonic numbers, and a short proof for the first step of the induction. (Minor complaint: is nicer when expressed directly with multichoose)
p. 260, on Euler-Mascheroni
here, they state a variety of situations in which it arises, but not any of the ways to represent it computably based on these.
p. 261, on Stirling's formula

one can refine Stirling's approximation with a power series, either by adding a finite number of terms from the Euler-Maclaurin series for \(\log\Gamma\) / or from its logarithm /. They use Stirling's approx. for \(8!=40320\), using the latter; \(\sqrt{\tau8}\left(\frac8e\right)^8\left(1+\frac1{12\cdot8}\right)=40318.045\ldots\) instead of \(\sqrt{\tau8}\left(\frac8e\right)^8e^{\frac1{12\cdot 8}}=40320.217\ldots\); the next term in the Euler-Maclaurin series is \(\frac{-1}{360n^3}\), while that of its exponential is \(\frac1{288n^2}\); at \(n=8\) this predicts the original version to have 1/10th the error, and indeed, its actual error is 8.974 times smaller. It becomes superior for \(n>1.493579\ldots\); why do they use one that is less precise and longer to write?

this is also an example of the phrasing typical of them;

A more exact form of Stirling's formula augments it proportionally by the fraction \(1/12n\)

'augments proportionally' sounds like an overly-literal translation of Euclid that turns lucid Greek into horribly encumbersome and ambiguous English, and there is again a horrible lack of details here.
