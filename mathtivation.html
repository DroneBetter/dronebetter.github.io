mathematics and motivation

thoughts and feelings

i consider myself an amateur mathematician before a student of maths, even though i am also the latter and have incurred a significant amount of debt to be so

i had a lot of problems during A-levels from which i distracted myself (with programming and olympiadry and other things that weren't part of the UK's A-level course and as such weren't going to be considered at all by universities)

part of this was because i was fundamentally unable to bring myself to engage with the teachers and the course; i was disliked by my maths teachers, and they ran a tight ship, by which i mean did not permit any time for the asking of questions not immediately helpful to the course.

the A-level syllabus is quite curious; it appears as though it's a single specification uniform across both state and private schools, but there is a pattern present in both maths and physics; private schools hire people with significantly more background in an area (ie. people with MScs or PhDs who command higher salaries), those people want to cover the 'most interesting' modules (that provide the most leeway for them to convey pieces of intuition and problem-solving and functionally complete grasps of cool cohesive theories), the state schools already teaching those get outcompeted and switch to the modules less conducive to that

this is why it's somewhat more difficult to find a state school covering additional pure maths or astrophysics, and why they almost always do mechanics and statistics

as this process iterates and the disparity entrenches itself, the state schools lose all motivation they might have had to teach by conveying intuition and theories, reducing maths into a collection of rote applications of facts, with the goal changed to learning the maximum number of facts

'plug and chug'

this is a term i've seen in math.se to deride "plug the parameters into the algorithm, run it manually, and write down the answer it churns out" questions; these are seen as the worst kind there, since they're almost always duplicates and almost never come with any demonstration of effort on the part of the asker, because any modicum of effort would be sufficient to proceed.

the dichotomy of plug-and-chug vs. interesting questions isn't inherently tied to the subjects of modules; there is a lot of interesting statistics to do (see the Wikipedia page on random permutation statistics), the methods for which are omitted, leading to the subject as taught being useful neither as a basis for further advancement of maths nor for blind practitionry.

what university seems to be like in the majority of the UK

my uni differs significantly from the average in a few ways, but i don't believe any are meaningful here; it's relatively strong in physical sciences and has its campus conjoined to a research park, and its administration makes poor financial decisions leading to layoffs while still paying the Vice Chancellor ≈9 times the median UK salary

the maths dept. seems pretty good for research in the subjects it specialises in, though i'm unqualified to assess it, being that it's the only one i've ever seen and most of the contents of research seminars are over my head; however, i can talk about the undergraduate course

i won't go over the contents so much as the omissions

the statistics module carries most of the same problems as A-levels

in particular, there is a set of univariate distributions to memorise, and the parameters are given their common names without explanation of why they bear those names; the very first thing i would write in an introduction to a distribution would be the idealised model that it describes exactly, but as soon as it gets to the gamma distribution this just stops!

It is a two-parameter family of continuous probability distributions. It has a shape parameter \(\alpha>0\) and a scale parameter \(\beta>0\). The gamma distribution is frequently a probability model for waiting times; for instance, in life testing, the waiting time until death is a random variable that is frequently modeled with a gamma distribution.

or

Student’s \(t\)-distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped, like the normal distribution, but has heavier tails, meaning that it is more prone to producing values that fall far from its mean; see Figure 1.1. [...] A final characteristic of the \(t\) distribution is that as \(\nu\) gets larger, the \(t\) gets closer to the standard normal.

these are somewhat okay qualitative descriptions but lack epistemological motivation and so become useless! what are they? what object is lifespan like, which the gamma function models perfectly? a student would be forgiven for imagining that research statistics is largely comprised of dreaming up new arbitrary arrangements of symbols into formulas and performing least-squares tests to see how well they fit sets of data

with linear algebra and complex analysis, i have found myself learning about things for my own work (residues, saddle point approximation, generalised eigenvectors) around a year before being 'taught' them, which makes the fact that i pay so much money to be here immensely frustrating
differential equations did have a few general methods taught somewhat conceptually, but also a lot of ad-hoc ones that felt like they were being included to make the syllabus larger as the ultimate end goal rather than to serve as useful tools for doing research; for certain very specific kinds of research they would, but for most they are superseded in an actual research context by use of computer algebra systmes

despite being so agonisingly aware of these problems, i'm unsure how to fix them; however, the two main causes are lack of passion among the teachers, and a focus on making exams that don't leave students behind (my uni's Wikipedia page notes its suspiciously high portion of first-class degrees awarded, which is not just sleight-of-hand but requires the point distributions themselves to be a certain way for their grade boundaries to meet regulations while being first-heavy). often the lecture notes are bastardisations of previous versions, that wantonly cut out pieces deemed unnecessary towards the teaching-testing cycle

friends' experiences in the minority of the UK

i have a lot of friends with interests in maths, whose unis are often more befitting to their interests, and i've discussed courses with them at great length; i've even had one disguise their coursework as questions borne out of genuine curiosity to try to get me to give them the critical piece of insight they were missing, and the fact that they could do this plausibly is a testament to good course design!

the vast unimaginable dissonance

i hold very strong conviction in some somewhat idiosyncratic views on what constitutes maths; for instance, it is possible to stumble upon 'deep' results by playing around in the Python shell, or PARI/GP or even WolframScript! (see my page on Notamathematician, who is particularly proficient at this)

i consider my page to be one of my most significant contributions to maths, and i arrived upon most of it years before writing that page; much of the pattern-recognition part (before getting to proofs), at least, i completed before even starting uni

and it frustrated me that all the skills necessary to complete it (that i learned on-the-fly) are so enormously distant from everything my uni has taught (which in comparison amount largely to a bunch of trivia interchangeable with any other trivia)

i sympathise greatly with Doron Zeilberger, whose opinions often revolve around what he terms experimental math, prioritising the discoveries above the formalisations and proofs, which he sees as ultimately secondary; i agree, that although endeavouring to prove something can lead to considering new things and developing new theory, it isn't the only way, and it shouldn't be uniquely prized on a pedestal all of its own and treated as the only way to approach things by textbooks

but teaching the art of meandering in a classroom environment is harder than in textbooks, especially when the class is comprised of people who haven't sought out to learn it and when you

but my uni experience has been only a vague shadow of the proof-based approach to maths; it is the plug-and-chug approach, which inherently cannot be maths and cannot produce mathematicians

less important things

problems specific to my school in particular

i had the experience of being offered to leave the foreign languages classes to study ICT instead.

ICT is a fundamentally broken subject here, moreso than all the others; its creation was based on the government deciding that computer literacy was important, then trusting Microsoft to define what computer literacy was (to which, of course, the biggest office program company answered "proficiency with office programs").

but the school used it to gerrymander the rabble-rousing students away so they'd stop distracting the others; i wasn't disruptive, but was unmotivated (explaining to my teachers that i saw languages as routine and pointless), so they sent me away too

however, they had no intention of finishing (or even really starting) the course; it was treated as a daycare, where the 'teacher' sat at her computer using Facebook and the 'students' played whatever Flash games they wanted, with her only piping up to tell them to be quiet when they grew too loud.

attainment-dependent funding exists, but only for English and maths; assumedly, this was done not for financial reasons but to reduce stress upon the teachers.

but it still is bizarre to me how this could be done by a school with near 2000 pupils!

it's exactly analogous to a company making a separate entity to transfer its debt to then shut down, which is fraud, and all the Ofsted inspections seem to have missed it!
